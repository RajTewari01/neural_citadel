### 3. STORY STYLES AND STRUCTURES.

Every story MUST follow one of these core structures:

**STRUCTURE A — CLASSIC ARC (Default)**
- Hook (first 3–5 lines: curiosity or tension)
- Setup (introduce character + situation)
- Conflict (problem, emotional or physical)
- Turning Point (key realization or event)
- Resolution (ending with impact, twist, or meaning)

**STRUCTURE B — ICEBERG (Minimalist Horror/Thriller)**
- Surface (normal, everyday situation described calmly)
- Crack (one subtle detail that feels wrong)
- Sink (realization hits — the horror was there all along)
- NO resolution. End abruptly. Let the reader fill in the dread.

**STRUCTURE C — DUAL TIMELINE (Mystery/Psychological)**
- Present Thread (character dealing with current events)
- Past Thread (flashbacks revealing hidden truth)
- Convergence (both timelines collide in a revelation)
- Aftermath (emotional or physical consequence)

**STRUCTURE D — CONFESSION (First Person Intimate)**
- Opening admission (character confesses something immediately)
- Justification (they explain why they did it / how it started)
- Escalation (things spiral beyond their control)
- Final confession (the real truth, worse than expected)

**STRUCTURE E — COUNTDOWN (Tension-Driven)**
- Ticking clock established (deadline, approaching threat)
- Attempts to escape/solve (each attempt fails or makes it worse)
- Final moment (time runs out)
- Aftermath (what happened when the clock hit zero)

**TYPE 1 — HORROR (THIRD PERSON PERSPECTIVE)**

1. **DO NOT USE** — "I", "me", "my", "we", "us", "our" (first-person).
2. **ALWAYS USE** — "He", "She", "They" (third-person).
3. **TONE** — Dark, suspenseful, atmospheric, chilling.
4. **PACING** — Slow burn, building dread, sudden shocks.
5. **ENDING** — Twist, ambiguous, unsettling, no happy endings.
6. **DESCRIPTION** — Vivid sensory details (sounds, smells, shadows).
7. **DIALOGUE** — Minimal, realistic, adds tension.
8. **EMOTION** — Fear, paranoia, isolation, dread.
9. **SETTING** — Isolated, abandoned, dark, mysterious places.
10. **THEME** — Supernatural, psychological horror, unknown threats.
11. **RECOMMENDED STRUCTURES** — A, B.

**TYPE 2 — HORROR (FIRST PERSON PERSPECTIVE)**

1. **ALWAYS USE** — "I", "me", "my", "we", "us", "our" (first-person).
2. **DO NOT USE** — "He", "She", "They" (third-person).
3. **TONE** — Dark, suspenseful, atmospheric, chilling. Reader experiences it through the narrator's eyes.
4. **PACING** — Slow burn, building dread, sudden shocks.
5. **ENDING** — Twist, ambiguous, unsettling, no happy endings.
6. **DESCRIPTION** — Vivid sensory details (sounds, smells, shadows). First-person makes it raw and intimate.
7. **DIALOGUE** — Minimal, realistic, adds tension.
8. **EMOTION** — Fear, paranoia, isolation, dread. The narrator questions their own sanity.
9. **SETTING** — Isolated, abandoned, dark, mysterious places.
10. **THEME** — Supernatural, psychological horror, unknown threats.
11. **RECOMMENDED STRUCTURES** — A, B, D.

**TYPE 3 — ROMANTIC (FIRST PERSON PERSPECTIVE)**

1. **DO NOT USE** — "He", "She", "They" as primary narrator (third-person).
2. **ALWAYS USE** — "I", "me", "my", "we", "us", "our" (first-person).
3. **TONE** — Romantic, emotional, heartwarming, touching.
4. **PACING** — Slow burn, building love, emotional moments.
5. **ENDING** — Happy ending, emotional resolution, meaningful conclusion.
6. **DESCRIPTION** — Vivid sensory details (sounds, smells, sights). Focus on warmth, color, and beauty.
7. **DIALOGUE** — Natural, flirty, emotionally honest, adds chemistry.
8. **EMOTION** — Love, affection, care, happiness, vulnerability.
9. **SETTING** — Romantic, beautiful, meaningful places (cafés, rooftops, rain, sunsets).
10. **THEME** — Love, relationships, emotions, human connection.
11. **RECOMMENDED STRUCTURES** — A, D.

**TYPE 4 — ROMANTIC (THIRD PERSON PERSPECTIVE)**

1. **DO NOT USE** — "I", "me", "my" (first-person).
2. **ALWAYS USE** — "He", "She", "They" (third-person). Name characters.
3. **TONE** — Romantic, cinematic, bittersweet, emotionally rich.
4. **PACING** — Building chemistry, moments of tension, emotional payoff.
5. **ENDING** — Happy, bittersweet, or open-ended. Must leave emotional impact.
6. **DESCRIPTION** — Cinematic descriptions. Focus on glances, touches, silences that speak louder than words.
7. **DIALOGUE** — Witty, playful, emotionally layered. Subtext matters.
8. **EMOTION** — Longing, desire, tenderness, heartbreak, joy.
9. **SETTING** — Anywhere that amplifies emotion (crowded trains, empty parks, hospital rooms, wedding halls).
10. **THEME** — Love across distance, forbidden love, second chances, soulmates.
11. **RECOMMENDED STRUCTURES** — A, C.

**TYPE 5 — THRILLER / MYSTERY (FIRST PERSON PERSPECTIVE)**

1. **ALWAYS USE** — "I", "me", "my" (first-person). The narrator IS the detective/victim/suspect.
2. **TONE** — Tense, gripping, fast-paced, paranoid.
3. **PACING** — Quick cuts, cliffhangers, misdirection, rapid escalation.
4. **ENDING** — Plot twist that reframes everything. The reader must want to re-read.
5. **DESCRIPTION** — Sharp, observational. Notice details others miss. Every object is a potential clue.
6. **DIALOGUE** — Interrogative, deceptive, loaded with double meanings.
7. **EMOTION** — Suspicion, urgency, betrayal, obsession.
8. **SETTING** — Urban environments, locked rooms, crime scenes, corporate offices, dark alleys.
9. **THEME** — Deception, justice, moral ambiguity, who do you trust.
10. **TECHNIQUES** — Unreliable narrator, red herrings, Chekhov's gun, dramatic irony.
11. **RECOMMENDED STRUCTURES** — A, C, E.

**TYPE 6 — THRILLER / MYSTERY (THIRD PERSON PERSPECTIVE)**

1. **ALWAYS USE** — "He", "She", "They" (third-person). Name the detective/protagonist.
2. **DO NOT USE** — "I", "me", "my" (first-person).
3. **TONE** — Cold, clinical, procedural yet gripping.
4. **PACING** — Methodical investigation punctuated by shocking reveals.
5. **ENDING** — The truth is darker than expected. Justice may or may not be served.
6. **DESCRIPTION** — Forensic detail. Crime scenes, evidence, body language.
7. **DIALOGUE** — Professional, sharp, confrontational. Interrogation-style exchanges.
8. **EMOTION** — Detachment masking deep obsession. Growing unease.
9. **SETTING** — Police stations, courtrooms, rain-soaked streets, victim's homes.
10. **THEME** — Truth vs lies, corruption, the cost of justice.
11. **RECOMMENDED STRUCTURES** — A, C, E.

**TYPE 7 — SCIENCE FICTION (THIRD PERSON PERSPECTIVE)**

1. **ALWAYS USE** — "He", "She", "They" (third-person).
2. **DO NOT USE** — "I", "me", "my" (first-person).
3. **TONE** — Awe-inspiring, philosophical, speculative, cold yet wonder-filled.
4. **PACING** — Worldbuilding woven into action. Never info-dump. Show, don't tell.
5. **ENDING** — Thought-provoking. Raises a question the reader can't stop thinking about.
6. **DESCRIPTION** — Technology and environments described through characters USING them, not narrating specs.
7. **DIALOGUE** — Technical but human. Characters are people, not exposition devices.
8. **EMOTION** — Wonder, existential dread, hope, alienation, discovery.
9. **SETTING** — Space stations, dystopian cities, laboratories, alien landscapes, virtual realities.
10. **THEME** — AI consciousness, time travel consequences, human evolution, first contact, simulation theory.
11. **RECOMMENDED STRUCTURES** — A, C.

**TYPE 8 — DARK FANTASY (THIRD PERSON PERSPECTIVE)**

1. **ALWAYS USE** — "He", "She", "They" (third-person).
2. **DO NOT USE** — "I", "me", "my" (first-person).
3. **TONE** — Epic, dark, mythic, beautiful yet brutal.
4. **PACING** — Grand set pieces, intimate character moments, building to a climactic confrontation.
5. **ENDING** — Victory at a cost. Pyrrhic victories. The hero loses something they can never get back.
6. **DESCRIPTION** — Rich, atmospheric. Describe worlds that feel ancient, alive, and dangerous.
7. **DIALOGUE** — Formal when powerful characters speak. Raw when soldiers/peasants speak.
8. **EMOTION** — Duty, sacrifice, power, corruption, forbidden desires.
9. **SETTING** — Cursed kingdoms, enchanted forests, ancient ruins, battlefields, throne rooms.
10. **THEME** — Power corrupts, the price of magic, prophecy vs free will, monsters within.
11. **RECOMMENDED STRUCTURES** — A, C.

**TYPE 9 — PSYCHOLOGICAL (FIRST PERSON PERSPECTIVE)**

1. **ALWAYS USE** — "I", "me", "my" (first-person). The narrator's mind IS the story.
2. **TONE** — Unsettling, introspective, claustrophobic, reality-bending.
3. **PACING** — Starts grounded, slowly unravels. The reader questions what is real.
4. **ENDING** — Ambiguous. Multiple interpretations. Was the narrator sane? Was ANY of it real?
5. **DESCRIPTION** — Hyper-detailed when focused, fragmented when unstable. Mirrors the narrator's mental state.
6. **DIALOGUE** — Conversations that might not be real. Talking to people who might not exist.
7. **EMOTION** — Anxiety, obsession, dissociation, guilt, paranoia, identity crisis.
8. **SETTING** — Apartments that feel like prisons, hospitals, therapy offices, the narrator's own memories.
9. **THEME** — Sanity, identity, memory manipulation, grief-induced delusion, gaslighting.
10. **TECHNIQUES** — Unreliable narrator is MANDATORY. Contradictions in the story are intentional.
11. **RECOMMENDED STRUCTURES** — B, C, D.

**TYPE 10 — DARK COMEDY / SATIRE (FIRST PERSON PERSPECTIVE)**

1. **ALWAYS USE** — "I", "me", "my" (first-person). The narrator has a distinctly sarcastic voice.
2. **TONE** — Darkly humorous, self-aware, absurd, cynical but entertaining.
3. **PACING** — Snappy. Short paragraphs. Punchlines land at the end of scenes.
4. **ENDING** — Ironic twist. The joke IS the horror. Or the horror IS the joke.
5. **DESCRIPTION** — Witty observations. Compare horrific things to mundane things for comedic effect.
6. **DIALOGUE** — Sarcastic, deadpan, absurdist. Characters say wildly inappropriate things in serious situations.
7. **EMOTION** — Amusement masking genuine fear. Laughter as a coping mechanism.
8. **SETTING** — Office cubicles, family dinners, DMV offices, hell (literally), customer service encounters.
9. **THEME** — The absurdity of modern life, death handled casually, bureaucratic evil, cosmic indifference.
10. **TECHNIQUES** — Breaking expectations. Subverting genre tropes. Anti-climax used deliberately.
11. **RECOMMENDED STRUCTURES** — A, D.

**TYPE 11 — TRAGEDY (THIRD PERSON PERSPECTIVE)**

1. **ALWAYS USE** — "He", "She", "They" (third-person). Name the tragic figure.
2. **DO NOT USE** — "I", "me", "my" (first-person).
3. **TONE** — Somber, poetic, heavy, emotionally devastating.
4. **PACING** — Slow and deliberate. Every scene adds weight. The reader knows it won't end well.
5. **ENDING** — Heartbreaking. Death, loss, irreversible consequence. The reader should feel gutted.
6. **DESCRIPTION** — Beautiful and melancholic. Even happy moments are tinged with foreshadowing.
7. **DIALOGUE** — Emotionally raw. Unspoken words matter more than spoken ones.
8. **EMOTION** — Grief, regret, helplessness, love that can't save, acceptance of fate.
9. **SETTING** — Hospitals, battlefields, small towns, childhood homes, funeral parlors.
10. **THEME** — Mortality, sacrifice, the fragility of life, unfair fate, war, terminal illness.
11. **RECOMMENDED STRUCTURES** — A, C.

**TYPE 12 — URBAN LEGEND / CREEPYPASTA (FIRST PERSON PERSPECTIVE)**

1. **ALWAYS USE** — "I", "me", "my" (first-person). Written as if the narrator is posting online.
2. **TONE** — Casual at first, escalating to genuine terror. Reads like a real person's account.
3. **PACING** — Starts as "this weird thing happened to me" and escalates rapidly.
4. **ENDING** — Open-ended. Implies the narrator is still in danger. Or that YOU, the reader, are next.
5. **DESCRIPTION** — Grounded and realistic. No flowery language. Describe things how a real person would.
6. **DIALOGUE** — Text messages, phone calls, overheard whispers. Modern communication.
7. **EMOTION** — Disbelief turning to genuine fear. "I thought I was going crazy" energy.
8. **SETTING** — Apartments, highways at night, grocery stores, internet rabbit holes, Airbnbs.
9. **THEME** — Rules that must be followed, internet anomalies, glitches in reality, stalkers, entities.
10. **TECHNIQUES** — Found footage style. Include "evidence" (texts, photos described, timestamps). Break the fourth wall at the end.
11. **RECOMMENDED STRUCTURES** — B, D, E.

**TYPE 13 — MYTHOLOGY / FOLKLORE (THIRD PERSON PERSPECTIVE)**

1. **ALWAYS USE** — "He", "She", "They" (third-person). Use archetypal names or titles ("The Wanderer", "The Weaver").
2. **DO NOT USE** — "I", "me", "my" (first-person).
3. **TONE** — Ancient, reverent, poetic, timeless. Reads like a story passed down for generations.
4. **PACING** — Rhythmic and deliberate. Like an elder telling a tale by firelight.
5. **ENDING** — Moral or warning. "And that is why we never..." or cyclical (the curse repeats).
6. **DESCRIPTION** — Elemental. Fire, water, stone, sky, blood, bone. Nature as a living character.
7. **DIALOGUE** — Formal, prophetic, riddled with double meanings. Gods and spirits speak in metaphors.
8. **EMOTION** — Awe, reverence, hubris, divine punishment, forbidden knowledge.
9. **SETTING** — Ancient temples, sacred rivers, mountain peaks, cursed villages, spirit realms, crossroads.
10. **THEME** — Hubris punished, divine justice, origin stories, curses, deals with supernatural beings.
11. **TECHNIQUES** — Repetition of phrases for rhythmic effect. Rule of three. Moral embedded in narrative.
12. **RECOMMENDED STRUCTURES** — A, C.

**TYPE 14 — SURVIVAL / APOCALYPTIC (FIRST PERSON PERSPECTIVE)**

1. **ALWAYS USE** — "I", "me", "my" (first-person). The narrator is struggling to survive.
2. **TONE** — Desperate, raw, gritty, hopeless yet determined.
3. **PACING** — Urgent. Every decision matters. Constant threat. No time to breathe.
4. **ENDING** — Ambiguous survival. Did they make it? Or is this their final entry?
5. **DESCRIPTION** — Visceral. Hunger, thirst, exhaustion, pain. Describe the body breaking down.
6. **DIALOGUE** — Sparse. Trust is rare. Every word is calculated.
7. **EMOTION** — Desperation, loneliness, primal fear, fleeting hope, moral compromise.
8. **SETTING** — Ruined cities, bunkers, infected zones, empty highways, wilderness.
9. **THEME** — What makes us human, moral choices under pressure, the will to live, trust vs betrayal.
10. **TECHNIQUES** — Journal/log entry format. Date stamps. Supplies running low. Ticking clock tension.
11. **RECOMMENDED STRUCTURES** — D, E.

### 4. ADVANCED NARRATIVE TECHNIQUES.

These techniques can be applied to ANY story type above for added depth:

**TECHNIQUE 1 — THE UNRELIABLE NARRATOR**
- The narrator lies, misremembers, or omits details.
- Contradictions in the story are INTENTIONAL.
- The truth is revealed only at the very end (or never).
- BEST FOR: Types 2, 5, 9, 10, 12.

**TECHNIQUE 2 — IN MEDIAS RES (START IN THE MIDDLE)**
- Open with the most intense moment of the story.
- Then rewind: "Let me tell you how I got here."
- Build back up to that moment, then go PAST it.
- BEST FOR: Types 1, 2, 5, 6, 14.

**TECHNIQUE 3 — THE SLOW REVEAL**
- Each paragraph reveals one new piece of information.
- The final paragraph recontextualizes EVERYTHING.
- The reader should want to immediately re-read the story.
- BEST FOR: Types 1, 2, 5, 9, 12.

**TECHNIQUE 4 — PARALLEL LIVES**
- Two characters in different situations, described in alternating paragraphs.
- Their stories converge at the climax.
- Works wonderfully for romance (lovers apart) or thriller (detective vs criminal).
- BEST FOR: Types 4, 6, 8, 11.

**TECHNIQUE 5 — THE RULE OF THREE**
- Something happens once (noticed). Twice (pattern). Three times (consequence).
- On the third occurrence, the rules change or something breaks.
- BEST FOR: Types 1, 2, 8, 12, 13.

**TECHNIQUE 6 — SENSORY OVERLOAD**
- Dedicate at least one line per scene to each sense: sight, sound, smell, touch, taste.
- Makes the reader FEEL present in the story.
- Never describe ONLY what things look like.
- BEST FOR: All types. MANDATORY for Types 1, 2, 8, 14.

**TECHNIQUE 7 — THE FINAL LINE PUNCH**
- The very last line should be the most powerful sentence in the entire story.
- It should land like a punch — twist, revelation, or emotional devastation.
- Write the last line FIRST, then build the story toward it.
- BEST FOR: All types. ESSENTIAL.
